\begin{abstract}
We evaluate OpenNARS-for-Applications (ONA) for longitudinal identity resolution on Icelandic historical census data spanning 220 years (1703--1920), while benchmarking it on standard entity resolution datasets (Abt--Buy, Amazon--Google, DBLP--ACM, DBLP--Scholar). NARS is a general-purpose AI framework designed to reason with limited knowledge and resources; its core is Non-Axiomatic Logic (NAL), a term-based logic. Our experiments show that this evidence-based symbolic matcher provides a transparent and auditable ER baseline while remaining competitive on ICE-ID and bibliographic ER benchmarks. In the deployed LLR scoring variant, each prediction is computed as a decomposable sum of named symbolic judgments with explicit evidence weights, so explanations follow directly from the scoring function. We report pairwise metrics, calibration sensitivity, and a deployment-faithful candidate-graph evaluation on ICE-ID, and compare against classical probabilistic and neural baselines across standard ER datasets.
\end{abstract}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% INTRODUCTION %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% 

\section{Introduction}

Linking historical census records is a core operation in research on social mobility, demographic change, migration, and epidemiology, but it remains difficult in practice because names vary across time, fields are missing, and administrative boundaries change \cite{ruggles2018historical, bailey2020}. Supervised record-linkage systems have improved performance in specific settings, including nineteenth-century U.S.\ censuses \cite{feigenbaum2016}, though the choice of training data and evaluation protocol significantly affects reported accuracy \cite{feigenbaum2025training}. Yet most benchmarked setups still simplify the problem by using short temporal windows, limited relational structure, and flat geographic representations \cite{papadakis2023}.

This paper asks whether a symbolic reasoning system can remain competitive in longitudinal identity resolution while improving transparency at the level of individual match decisions and error analysis. We evaluate a Non-Axiomatic Reasoning System (NARS), implemented with OpenNARS-for-Applications (ONA), on ICE-ID and compare it against standard entity resolution (ER) benchmarks. ICE-ID provides a long-horizon test setting: it spans 16 Icelandic census waves from 1703 to 1920, contains 984,028 records, and includes 226,864 expert-curated person clusters. It introduces temporal drift, hierarchical geography, and recurring patronymic name collisions, which makes it a useful benchmark for both symbolic and non-symbolic matchers. ICE-ID also complements existing historical and longitudinal linkage infrastructures such as the IPUMS linked samples \cite{ipums_lrs, ipums_mlp} and the North Atlantic Population Project \cite{ipums_napp, sobek2011big}.

We study two tasks on ICE-ID:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Intra-census linkage}: determine whether two records from the same census refer to the same person.
  \item \textbf{Cross-census linkage}: determine whether two records from different censuses refer to the same person despite spelling variation, age progression, and movement across locations.
\end{enumerate}

All ICE-ID experiments use a strictly temporal split: records up to 1870 for training, 1871--1890 for validation, and 1891--1920 for testing. We report pairwise metrics (precision, recall, F$_1$, ROC-AUC) and clustering metrics (Adjusted Rand Index, ARI) to separate local pair decisions from global entity consistency.

We compare three families of approaches under this benchmarking setup:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Classical probabilistic linkage}: Fellegi--Sunter and deterministic rule-based matchers;
  \item \emph{Symbolic reasoning}: NARS, which scores record pairs through accumulated evidence over named symbolic judgments, yielding a score that can be decomposed into auditable positive and negative contributions;
  \item \emph{Neural and LLM-based matchers}: Ditto \cite{Li2020DeepEM}, HierGAT \cite{yao2022hiergat}, zero-shot methods such as AnyMatch \cite{zhang2024anymatch}, and LLM-based matching methods (including the MatchGPT-style baseline used in our experiments), which can be strong predictors but do not expose the deployed score in the same symbolic evidence vocabulary.
\end{itemize}

Our results show that NARS provides a competitive and intrinsically interpretable baseline. On ICE-ID, it reaches pairwise performance comparable to strong probabilistic linkage baselines and close to the best deep learning result reported in our benchmark suite, while preserving a decomposable decision trace for each pair through explicit judgment contributions. On standard ER datasets, NARS performs especially well on bibliographic matching (DBLP--ACM, DBLP--Scholar), while remaining weaker on product-matching datasets (Abt--Buy, Amazon--Google), where long noisy text favors learned representations. These results support the use of symbolic reasoning as a practical ER baseline when auditability is a first-class requirement and motivate hybrid pipelines that combine symbolic evidence with neural representations.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{ICE-ID Benchmark}
We evaluate on ICE-ID \cite{iceid2024}, a benchmark of 984,028 Icelandic census records (1703--1920) with 226,864 expert-labeled person clusters. We use the canonical temporal splits (up to 1870 train / 1871--1890 validation / 1891--1920 test) and report pairwise metrics (Precision, Recall, F$_1$, AUC), clustering metrics (ARI, B$^3$), and ranking metrics (P@k, R@k). Full dataset documentation follows the datasheet and data-statement reporting norms of \citet{gebru2021datasheets} and \citet{Bender2018DataSF} and is provided in the companion data paper.

\subsubsection{Data Preparation}
Records are loaded with string columns (names, status labels) normalized to lowercase and numeric fields coerced to integers.
The key numeric columns are
\texttt{id} (unique record identifier),
\texttt{heimild} (census wave, i.e.\ the year of enumeration),
\texttt{birthyear} (year of birth),
\texttt{person} (expert-curated identity label linking records of the same individual),
and geographic/kinship foreign keys (\texttt{farm}, \texttt{parish}, \texttt{district}, \texttt{county}, \texttt{partner}, \texttt{father}, \texttt{mother}).
Missing IDs are dropped; missing name fields are filled with empty strings. NARS and the symbolic matchers operate directly on these record pairs; the machine learning (ML) ensemble baseline uses pairwise TF-IDF features over concatenated record text. All ICE-ID experiments under the shared protocol use the same raw record representation and temporal splits keyed by \texttt{heimild} (census wave).

\subsubsection{Pair Construction and Sampling}
Positive pairs are drawn from expert-labeled clusters: two records are labeled as a match if they share the same \texttt{person} ID. For \emph{cross-wave} linkage, pair generation spans distinct census-year windows while preserving the same cluster semantics. Negative pairs are sampled from records assigned to different clusters, using a 2:1 negative-to-positive ratio within each split.

For ICE-ID, pair generation and any subsampling are performed \emph{inside} the fixed temporal splits (train: up to 1870, validation: 1871--1890, test: 1891--1920); no random split is applied across years. If a split contains more than 50,000 candidate pairs, we uniformly subsample to that cap so that NARS and the ML baselines are evaluated on the same bounded pair set. The cap is configurable, and the implementation does not impose a same-census-year constraint on negatives; within-wave evaluation can be obtained by restricting the record pool to a single \texttt{heimild} in a given run.

\subsection{ML Ensemble Baseline}

As a baseline, we compare against an ensemble of four tree-based classifiers (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, Random Forest) averaging match probabilities. The ensemble is trained on pairwise text-derived and structured comparison features computed from the same raw record pairs used by NARS. Model selection uses 10-fold cross-validation within the training split; final evaluation uses the same held-out validation and test splits as NARS.

\subsection{OpenNARS-for-Applications}

\textbf{Implementation:} We use OpenNARS-for-Applications (ONA) \cite{hammer} as the underlying NARS engine, building on the Non-Axiomatic Logic (NAL) framework \cite{wang}. ONA defines the judgment vocabulary and evidence framework; record pairs are converted into Narsese statements encoding attribute agreements and disagreements. The reported experiments use the LLR scoring instantiation described in the ``Practical scoring variant'' paragraph below.

\textbf{Algorithm Overview:} Figure~\ref{fig:algo} summarizes the procedure.

This pseudocode illustrates the conceptual pattern-pool formulation for intuition; the reported implementation replaces Step~3's revision-based scoring with the LLR scorer described below.

\textbf{Complexity:} Conceptual pattern-pool scoring is $O(N \cdot n \cdot |J|)$ where $N$ = pairs, $n$ = reference patterns, $|J|$ = judgments per pair; the LLR variant reduces to $O(N \cdot |J|)$ since each judgment maps to a precomputed log-ratio.

The LLR scoring pipeline used in all reported experiments proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Judgment Generation.} A preprocessing function converts each record pair $(r_1, r_2)$ into a set of atomic judgments---e.g., \texttt{same\_nafn\_norm} (normalized full-name match; \emph{nafn} is Icelandic for ``name'') and \texttt{birthyear\_close} (birth years within 5~years)---based on attribute comparisons for name, birth year, sex, census wave (\texttt{heimild}), and location.
    \item \textbf{Evidence Accumulation.} During training, each labeled pair increments per-judgment positive or negative counts (with Laplace $+1$ smoothing). The accumulated counts yield a log-likelihood ratio $\mathrm{LLR}_j = \log\frac{P(j \mid \text{match})}{P(j \mid \text{non-match})}$ for every judgment type $j$.
    \item \textbf{Pair Scoring.} For a new pair, the generated judgments are looked up in the LLR table, summed with a base-rate log-prior, and passed through a sigmoid to produce a match probability.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Interpretability of the deployed scorer.} In the LLR pipeline, the pair score is an additive function of named judgment tokens, so each prediction can be decomposed into exact per-judgment contributions to the final log-odds. This makes local explanations (why a pair matched), global analysis (which judgments dominate), and counterfactual inspection (how the score changes when a judgment is removed) native to the deployed model rather than a separate post-hoc analysis layer.

\subsubsection{Conceptual NARS Formulation (Background)}
\label{subsec:conceptual_nars}
The following pattern, pattern-pool, and revision rules describe the full conceptual NARS formulation that motivates the design and establish the correspondence with Non-Axiomatic Logic (see Appendix~\ref{subsec:nars_background}). The reported experiments use the LLR scoring pipeline above, which is a practical instantiation of this NARS framework that replaces the explicit pattern-pool revision step with precomputed per-judgment log-likelihood ratios.

\textbf{Pattern.} A pair of census records yields an atomic pattern: the unordered set of all generated judgments, annotated with truth-value $(1, 0.9)$ if the records belong to the same individual or $(0, 0.9)$ otherwise.

\textbf{Pattern Pool.} The pattern pool contains patterns, sorted in ascending order of the expectation of the truth-value.

\textbf{Inference Rule.} Given two patterns $p_1$ and $p_2$ containing judgment sets $j_1$ and $j_2$ with truth-values $t_1$ and $t_2$, the proposed inference rule derives three new patterns:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The judgment set of the new pattern is $j_1 \setminus j_2$, and the truth-value is $t_1$.
    \item The judgment set of the new pattern is $j_2 \setminus j_1$, and the truth-value is $t_2$.
    \item The judgment set of the new pattern is $j_1 \cap j_2$, and the truth-value is $\mathrm{revise}(t_1, t_2)$.
\end{enumerate}

These rules ensure that each derived truth-value is inherited from parent evidence rather than fabricated. In case 1, evidence contributing to $t_1$ also supports the new sub-pattern; its truth-value may later be revised with independently obtained patterns. Case 2 is symmetric.

In case 3, the intersection pattern is a sub-pattern of both parents, so both $t_1$ and $t_2$ contribute evidence and revision merges them. To avoid double-counting, only patterns derived from independent evidence sources may participate in a single revision step.

\textbf{Learning as Recognition.} In the conceptual formulation, the system constructs a query pattern and measures its overlap with existing patterns in the pool. The degree of overlap determines the match score. During this process, new patterns may be added to the pool and existing truth-values may be updated.

In the conceptual formulation, the final expectation score can be thresholded to obtain a binary match decision. In the reported implementation, threshold calibration is performed in the deployed LLR pipeline described below.


\emph{Conceptual truth-value scoring (not used in reported experiments).} At scoring time, up to $n$ reference patterns are retrieved from the pool---half with the highest expectation (likely matches) and half with the lowest (likely non-matches)---to provide balanced evidence. For each reference pattern, a pairwise truth-value is computed: frequency $f$ equals the size of the judgment intersection divided by the size of the longer pattern, and confidence $c$ is inherited from the longer (more specific) pattern. These pairwise truth-values are then merged via revision, and the final expectation $e$ serves as the match score: higher $e$ indicates stronger evidence that the two records belong to the same individual.

\textbf{Practical scoring variant (summary).} The LLR pipeline described above replaces truth-value revision with per-judgment log-likelihood ratios (Laplace-smoothed). The pair score is $\sigma\!\bigl(\sum_j \mathrm{LLR}_j + \log\tfrac{p}{1-p}\bigr)$, where $\sigma$ is the logistic sigmoid and $p$ is the training-set base rate. Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams} documents the parameters.

\subsubsection{Threshold Calibration (Deployed LLR Pipeline)}
\label{subsec:threshold_calibration}

To convert LLR-based match probabilities into binary decisions, we calibrate a threshold on labeled validation pairs. For ICE-ID, after accumulating LLR statistics from training pairs, we score the held-out validation set to obtain separate positive and negative score distributions. We then set
\[
  \tau \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{median}(\text{pos\_scores}) + \mathrm{median}(\text{neg\_scores})}{2}.
\]
At test time, any pair with score~$\ge\tau$ is predicted as a match, otherwise as a non-match. For classic ER benchmarks, we instead select $\tau$ to maximize validation F$_1$ to handle extreme class imbalance.

\textbf{Calibration Split:} For ICE-ID, we compute $\tau$ on the held-out validation pairs, ensuring no leakage from test data.

\subsection{ICE-ID Results}\label{sec:NARSResults}

Table~\ref{tab:iceid_results} summarizes pairwise performance on ICE-ID. Under our shared evaluation protocol, NARS and Fellegi--Sunter both reach F$_1$=0.994; classical probabilistic linkage is surprisingly competitive on this well-structured dataset \cite{fellegi1969theory, Sayers2015ProbabilisticRL, Newcombe1959AutomaticLO}. Ditto reports the highest F$_1$ (0.997) under its own data preparation and splits, while HierGAT (0.857) underperforms under its separate pipeline. AnyMatch transfers well without dataset-specific training (0.907). The NARS result (F$_1$=0.994, AUC=0.998) shows that symbolic judgment-based evidence captures most of the discriminative signal in the pairwise benchmark setting while preserving a fully decomposable score for each prediction. MatchGPT was evaluated on only 50 pairs per dataset, so its scores should be interpreted with caution.

\subsection{Pairwise--Cluster Mismatch}
\label{sec:sanity_checks}

Clustering and ranking metrics can diverge from pairwise classification metrics because they depend on the evaluation graph, not only on local pair decisions. In the pairwise benchmark, pairs are sampled independently rather than scored on a full deployment-style candidate graph, so connected-component clustering is computed on an induced sampled graph and cannot fully enforce transitivity. As a result, ARI-CC may differ substantially from what pairwise F$_1$ suggests. On ICE-ID, the pairwise benchmark still yields ARI-CC=0.881 (Table~\ref{tab:nars_horizontal}), but this should not be compared directly to the candidate-graph clustering results in Table~\ref{tab:nars_graph_eval}. Likewise, P@k depends on score ordering, not just the decision threshold, so a model can achieve high F$_1$ while ranking pairs poorly.

A random scorer on the same sampled graph confirms this: it yields ARI-CC~$\approx 0$ and P@k~$\approx$ the positive rate, matching the behavior expected under sparse sampling. This is a protocol artifact, not an implementation error.

\textbf{Implication.} High pairwise metrics do not guarantee good clustering or ranking performance. Entity resolution pipelines should be evaluated end-to-end on the downstream task (clustering on a candidate graph) rather than on pairwise classification alone. We present such a graph-based evaluation for NARS in the next section.

\textbf{Per-pair decomposition.} Figure~\ref{fig:nars_waterfall} illustrates the additive score decomposition for a true-positive pair from the ICE-ID test set. \textbf{Error diagnosis via decomposition.} Figure~\ref{fig:nars_fp_waterfall} shows the same decomposition for a \emph{false positive}: two different men both named \emph{Magn\'us Magn\'usson} in the 1835 census, living in different counties with a 20-year birth-year gap (1809 vs.\ 1829). Three name judgments (\texttt{same\_nafn\_norm} $+5.00$, \texttt{same\_first\_name} $+4.30$, \texttt{same\_patronym} $+3.73$) contribute $+13.03$ to the log-odds, overwhelming the geographic and birth-year counter-evidence ($-10.92$). The net log-odds of $+2.12$ yields $\sigma(+2.12)=0.893$, above the threshold. The decomposition immediately diagnoses the failure mode: Icelandic patronymic naming produces common name collisions (``Magn\'us, son of Magn\'us''), and the current judgment vocabulary does not discount high-frequency names. A domain expert inspecting this breakdown could propose frequency-weighted name judgments or a stronger birth-year penalty as targeted fixes---a debugging workflow that is not available when the scorer is a black-box neural model.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/nars_fp_explanation.pdf}
  \caption{Per-judgment LLR decomposition for a false-positive ICE-ID pair (two distinct individuals both named Magn\'us Magn\'usson, census 1835). Name evidence overwhelms geographic and birth-year counter-evidence, producing $\sigma(+2.12)=0.893$.}

This decomposition contrasts with neural matchers, where the match probability is produced by a learned latent scoring function rather than an explicit symbolic evidence sum. Ditto outputs a single probability from a fine-tuned language model, and HierGAT produces scores through learned graph-based representations. In such models, pair-level explanations are typically obtained through additional interpretation methods (for example, attention inspection or feature-attribution tools) rather than directly from the deployed scoring function. NARS provides this decomposition natively: the LLR weight of each judgment is the exact contribution that judgment makes to the final log-odds, and any decision can be audited by inspecting the breakdown. This property is particularly valuable in historical record linkage, where scholars need to understand and validate linkage decisions before drawing substantive conclusions about demographic or genealogical patterns.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% DISCUSSION %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Discussion \& Conclusion}\label{sec:Discussion_Conclusion}

Our calibrated NARS pipeline (median-midpoint thresholding on ICE-ID and validation-F$_1$ thresholding on classic ER datasets) shows that a symbolic, evidence-based matcher can achieve competitive pairwise accuracy in entity resolution without deep representation learning or GPU resources, while keeping the deployed scoring path directly inspectable. On the balanced ICE-ID pairwise benchmark, NARS reaches F$_1$=0.994, matching Fellegi--Sunter and trailing Ditto (0.997) by a small margin under separate evaluation pipelines. Across the collected benchmark runs, NARS records the highest F$_1$ on four of five reported cross-dataset evaluations, and on the standard ER benchmarks it matches or exceeds prior reported supervised results on DBLP--ACM and DBLP--Scholar. The main advantage is not uniform dominance in F$_1$, but that each decision is expressed as an auditable sum of named evidence terms with explicit weights.

Fellegi--Sunter matches NARS on ICE-ID (0.994) but degrades on two-table benchmarks (e.g., 0.126 on Abt--Buy vs.\ NARS's 0.648). This contrast highlights the role of domain-adaptive preprocessing in NARS: dataset-specific symbolic judgments transfer across ER schemas more effectively than a fixed set of classical comparison rules designed for a structured census setting.

Our results also reveal important limitations. The candidate-graph evaluation (Table~\ref{tab:nars_graph_eval}) shows that NARS produces excellent score rankings (AUC rounds to 1.000; P@k=R@k=0.961), but converting those scores into hard decisions on a realistic graph (positive rate $\approx$1.3\%) remains difficult: threshold-dependent F$_1$ drops to 0.473 and connected-component clustering over-merges (B$^3$ precision=0.218). This gap between ranking quality and hard-decision quality is a known entity-resolution challenge \cite{Christen2012ASO} and motivates graph-aware post-processing, including Bayesian partition priors \cite{marchant2023bayesian}, knowledge-driven graph models \cite{hu2025grapher}, and progressive pay-as-you-go strategies \cite{payasyougo}. Learned blocking schemes \cite{Michelson2006LearningBS} may also improve candidate generation.

The ablation results (Table~\ref{tab:ablations}) show that name judgments are by far the most informative feature category ($\Delta$F$_1$=$-$0.078), while geographic judgments have a small effect and the remaining categories (birthyear, sex, census year) show negligible individual impact at the high baseline F$_1$=0.994. The substantial redundancy among non-name features suggests that the model relies primarily on name matching, with other features serving as tiebreakers.

In conclusion, NARS demonstrates that symbolic reasoning can serve as a transparent, competitive baseline for entity resolution. As shown in Section~\ref{sec:interpretability}, the deployed LLR scorer supports exact per-judgment decomposition of each prediction, which lets domain experts inspect and audit individual match decisions directly. Combined with low resource requirements, this makes NARS a useful complement to neural matchers in hybrid ER pipelines, especially in settings where auditability and error analysis are part of the core workflow.

\subsection{Benchmark-specific NARS preprocessing (classic ER datasets)}
ICE-ID preprocessing remains unchanged and follows the rules described earlier in the paper. For the classic two-table ER benchmarks, we replaced the generic token-overlap preprocessing with dataset-specific judgments so that NARS can use the fields that define matches in each dataset.

\textbf{Shared normalization and scoring.} Text fields are lowercased, stripped, and normalized by removing non-alphanumeric characters and collapsing whitespace. Similarity is the maximum of token Jaccard overlap and SequenceMatcher ratio. For each text field we emit:
\texttt{field\_exact} if normalized strings match, else
\texttt{field\_sim\_high} ($\ge$ 0.90),
\texttt{field\_sim\_med} ($\ge$ 0.75),
\texttt{field\_sim\_low} ($\ge$ 0.50), or
\texttt{field\_sim\_vlow} (otherwise).
Numeric fields are parsed by extracting the first numeric token. We emit \texttt{field\_exact} if absolute difference $\le$ 0.01, \texttt{field\_close} if absolute difference $\le$ a dataset-specific threshold or relative difference $\le$ 0.05, and \texttt{field\_far} if relative difference $\ge$ 0.20. Year fields are parsed from 4-digit years and emit \texttt{field\_same} (diff=0), \texttt{field\_close} (diff $\le$ 1), or \texttt{field\_far}. Time fields accept seconds or \texttt{mm:ss} and emit \texttt{time\_same} (diff=0), \texttt{time\_close} (diff $\le$ 5s), or \texttt{time\_far}. Phone fields compare digits only and emit \texttt{phone\_exact}, \texttt{phone\_last7}, or \texttt{phone\_mismatch}. Address numbers emit \texttt{addr\_num\_match} or \texttt{addr\_num\_diff} when parseable.

\textbf{Dataset-specific judgments.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Abt--Buy:} \texttt{name}, \texttt{description} (text), \texttt{price} (numeric; abs $\le$ 1.0 or rel $\le$ 0.05).
  \item \textbf{Amazon--Google:} \texttt{title}, \texttt{manufacturer} (text), \texttt{price} (numeric; abs $\le$ 1.0 or rel $\le$ 0.05).
  \item \textbf{DBLP--ACM / DBLP--Scholar:} \texttt{title}, \texttt{authors}, \texttt{venue} (text), \texttt{year} (same/close/far).
  \item \textbf{iTunes--Amazon:} \texttt{Song\_Name}, \texttt{Artist\_Name}, \texttt{Album\_Name}, \texttt{Genre} (text), \texttt{Price} (numeric; abs $\le$ 0.5 or rel $\le$ 0.05), \texttt{Time} (time), \texttt{Released} and \texttt{CopyRight} (year).
  \item \textbf{Walmart--Amazon:} \texttt{title}, \texttt{category}, \texttt{brand}, \texttt{modelno} (text), \texttt{price} (numeric; abs $\le$ 1.0 or rel $\le$ 0.05).
  \item \textbf{Beer:} \texttt{Beer\_Name}, \texttt{Brew\_Factory\_Name}, \texttt{Style} (text), \texttt{ABV} (numeric; abs $\le$ 0.3 or rel $\le$ 0.05).
  \item \textbf{Fodors--Zagats:} \texttt{name}, \texttt{addr}, \texttt{city}, \texttt{type}, \texttt{class} (text), plus phone and address-number judgments.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Data alignment for DeepMatcher splits.} For these benchmarks, pair IDs correspond to the source tables (e.g., \texttt{abt.csv}/\texttt{buy.csv}, \texttt{amazon.csv}/\texttt{google.csv}), so we align record IDs to the pair indices before applying the standard right-table offset to make IDs globally unique. This ensures that all training and evaluation pairs map to actual records.

\textbf{Thresholding.} ICE-ID uses the original median-based separation of positive/negative validation scores. For classic ER benchmarks, we select a per-dataset threshold that maximizes validation F$_1$ to accommodate extreme class imbalance.

\subsection{NARS Background}
\label{subsec:nars_background}
NARS \cite{wang} uses \emph{channels} to transform external information into Narsese (its internal knowledge representation), as implemented in OpenNARS \cite{hammer}. A channel can process information of any modality---strings for natural language, matrices for visual signals---under a single principle: compounding atomic inputs (e.g., words in a sentence or pixels in an image) into structured terms. For the problem discussed here, channels process census records.

Compounding is guided by NARS's memory-based attention allocation rather than brute-force enumeration. However, when only census data are provided, NARS lacks the semantics of domain concepts (e.g., farm locations), so additional preprocessing of raw records is required. Future work could supply spatial and temporal relationships directly, allowing NARS to ground input semantics through geographical and historical context.

In summary, our approach adapts the compound generation method from NARS channels and the truth-value calculus to entity resolution, while omitting memory management and goal-driven inference components. This focused adaptation enables direct comparison with standard ML baselines on ER benchmarks.

\subsubsection{Channel}

The NARS channel maintains a short-term cache of recent inputs, each consisting of multiple atomic sensations. From this cache the channel constructs two types of compounds: \emph{spatial compounds}, which group co-occurring sensations into a single moment, and \emph{temporal compounds}, which capture implication relationships between spatial compounds for prediction. Because census records lack short-term sequential structure, we use only spatial compounds.
Rather than enumerating all combinations of atomic sensations, the channel forms a compound as a whole and then determines which parts can be eliminated.

\subsubsection{Truth-Value}

In NARS, the truth-value of a judgment quantifies both positive and negative evidence \cite{wang}. Given $w_+$ units of positive evidence and $w_-$ units of negative evidence, the truth-value $(f, c)$ is defined as $f=w_+/(w_++w_-)$ and $c=(w_++w_-)/(w_++w_-+k)$, where $f \in [0,1]$ (the \emph{frequency}) is the proportion of positive evidence and $c \in [0,1]$ (the \emph{confidence}) measures how much existing evidence there is relative to $k$ hypothetical future observations. Higher $f$ indicates stronger positive support; higher $c$ indicates that $f$ is less likely to change with new evidence.

\subsubsection{The Expectation of Truth-Values}

Because the truth-value is two-dimensional, NARS defines a scalar summary called \emph{expectation}: $e = c(f-0.5) + 0.5$, with $e\in [0,1]$. Values of $e$ near 1 indicate a judgment that is both true and reliable; values near 0 indicate a judgment that is both false and reliable. When $e \approx 0.5$, the system is either ignorant ($f \approx 0.5$) or lacks sufficient evidence ($c$ is low).

\subsubsection{The Revision of Truth-Values}

When two independent sources provide truth-values for the same judgment, NARS integrates them via \emph{revision}: $w_+=w_+^{(1)}+w_+^{(2)}$, $w_-=w_-^{(1)}+w_-^{(2)}$, with $f$ and $c$ recomputed from the pooled evidence counts.

\subsubsection{Pilot Hyperparameter Tuning}

\emph{Note: this subsection describes an early pilot procedure used to select NARS hyperparameters before the final benchmark protocol (Section~\ref{sec:methods}) was fixed.}

Census records up to 1899 served as training data and records after 1899 as test data. Records were sorted by individual so that records from the same person appeared consecutively. For each record, $m$ subsequent rows were paired with it, yielding both positive pairs (same person) and negative pairs (different persons).

At test time, $x$ individuals were sampled from the test set and all pairwise match scores among their records were computed. A symmetric threshold $\delta$ around 0.5 was swept: pairs with score $> 0.5 + \delta$ were predicted as matches and pairs with score $< 0.5 - \delta$ as non-matches, and F$_1$ was computed for each $\delta$. Default settings: $1/10{,}000$ of training data, $x=1{,}000$ test individuals, $m=5$ neighbor rows, and 10 reference patterns.

Increasing the number of reference patterns improved reliability, while varying the training proportion and observation scope had less consistent effects.

\textbf{Handcrafted Rules (pilot-era rule set).} The following field-level judgments were used in early pilot experiments. The final benchmark uses the extended judgment vocabulary described in Section~\ref{sec:methods} (which adds graded birthyear thresholds, geographic hierarchy levels, and temporal gap judgments). When processing a record pair, the pilot rules generate:

\textit{\textbf{Heimild}}: Emits \texttt{differ\_in\_X\_years}, where $X$ is the absolute difference in census year between the two records.

\textit{\textbf{nafn\_norm}}: Emits \texttt{same\_nafn\_norm} or \texttt{different\_nafn\_norm} based on whether the normalized full names match. The same logic applies to \textbf{\textit{first\_name}}, \textbf{\textit{patronym}}, and \textbf{\textit{surname}}.

\textit{\textbf{Birthyear}}: Emits \texttt{same\_birthyear} or \texttt{different\_birthyear} based on whether the birth years match.

\textit{\textbf{Sex}}: Emits \texttt{same\_sex} or \texttt{different\_sex} based on whether the sex values match.

\textit{\textbf{Status}}: Because social status is categorical rather than binary, two judgments are emitted: \texttt{status\_is\_X} and \texttt{status\_is\_Y}, where $X$ and $Y$ are the status values from each record (unordered).

\textit{\textbf{Marriagestatus}}: Emits \texttt{same\_marriagestatus} or \texttt{different\_marriagestatus} based on whether the marital status values match.

\textit{\textbf{Farm}}: Emits \texttt{same\_farm} or \texttt{different\_farm} based on whether the farm IDs match. The same logic applies to \textit{\textbf{county}}, \textit{\textbf{parish}}, and \textit{\textbf{district}}.

\textit{\textbf{Label}}: The ground-truth indicator: records are from the same individual if and only if they share the same \texttt{person} ID.
